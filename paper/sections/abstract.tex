\begin{abstract}
Deep learning models deployed in safety-critical settings can fail silently under distribution shift, producing confident but unreliable outputs with no way to self-diagnose the failure. We study this in seismic phase picking, the task of identifying earthquake wave arrivals in seismograms. PhaseNet, a widely-deployed model trained on Northern California data, sees its P-wave detection rate collapse from 83.9\% to 35.1\% when deployed to Kazakhstan, a seismically active but data-sparse region. Despite this collapse, confidence scores and discrimination ability (AUROC) are near-identical across regions, so confidence-based deferral has no signal that performance has degraded. We develop a regime-aware deferral framework that provides the signal model confidence cannot. An HMM classifies the seismic environment into regimes and a Beta-Bernoulli model estimates how much evidence exists about PhaseNet's reliability in each. The combined score suppresses confidence when regime evidence is sparse, routing picks from poorly-observed periods for analyst review. At a 25\% deferral rate, the framework defers 90.9\% of picks made during seismically active periods versus 36.4\% for confidence-only deferral, directing analyst time toward periods of greatest environmental uncertainty. Confidence-only achieves higher system accuracy at matched rates (0.651 vs 0.619), as it targets the weakest individual picks. The combined score's value is in identifying when the system needs oversight, not which picks are weakest.
\end{abstract}
