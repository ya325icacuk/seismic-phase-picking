\begin{abstract}
Deep learning models deployed in safety-critical settings can fail silently under distribution shift, producing confident but unreliable outputs with no way to self-diagnose the failure. We study this in seismic phase picking, the task of identifying earthquake wave arrivals in seismograms. PhaseNet, a widely-deployed model trained on Northern California data, sees its P-wave detection rate collapse from 83.9\% to 35.1\% when deployed to Kazakhstan, a seismically active but data-sparse region where automated tools are most needed yet least validated. PhaseNet's confidence scores are near-identical across regions, so confidence alone cannot flag the collapse. We develop a regime-aware deferral framework that combines an external Hidden Markov Model of the seismic environment with Beta-Bernoulli estimation of PhaseNet's per-regime accuracy. The framework defers 90.9\% of picks made during seismically active periods, compared to 36.4\% when deferring on confidence alone, directing scarce analyst time toward regimes where reliability rests on the fewest observations, at a marginal cost in accuracy (0.619 vs 0.651). Data-sparse regions stand to benefit most from this framework, as they are precisely where automated tools have not been tested against local ground truth, making deferral mechanisms essential for ensuring they receive the same monitoring safeguards as data-rich ones.
\end{abstract}
