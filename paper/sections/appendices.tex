\subsection{Kazakhstan Station Selection}
\label{app:kz_stations}

The Kazakhstan-network station selection began with all 29 three-component broadband (BH*) stations active during 2020--2023, retrieved from IRIS FDSN. Four sequential filters were applied: (1) experimental and temporary arrays were removed (BAL5, BAL6, DEG1, DEG2, DEG4, DEG5, SAU1, SAU2), leaving 21, (2) waveform availability was confirmed at four test windows spanning the study period, leaving 12, (3) stations with sensor changes during 2020--2023 were excluded, and (4) KUR21 was dropped after yielding zero ISC-reviewed picks. This produced 11 stations, all sampling at 40\,Hz. Full metadata for the retained stations are given in \cref{tab:stations}.

Although the 11 stations span six sensor types, hardware heterogeneity is acceptable because each station's instrumentation remains stable within the study window, ensuring any shifts in PhaseNet's performance are more likely explained by seismic regime than sensor changes. \subsection{Event Catalog}
\label{app:catalog}

The ISC catalog was queried for M$\geq$3.0 events in Central Asia (41--55$^\circ$N, 55--85$^\circ$E) for 2020--2023, yielding 6{,}763 events. The ISC is preferred over the USGS NEIC because it integrates phase data from regional agencies including KNDC, giving more accurate hypocentral solutions for Central Asian events. The M$\geq$3.0 threshold sits slightly below the network's estimated completeness magnitude of M3.5, preserving the background seismicity rate information the HMM needs to distinguish Quiet, Active, and Decaying regimes. The magnitude distribution is heavily right-skewed: 6{,}763 events with M$\geq$3.0, 98 with M$\geq$4.0, 12 with M$\geq$4.5, and 3 with M$\geq$5.0. 

Regime transitions were triggered by M$\geq$4.5 events within 200\,km of a station.  Of the 12 qualifying catalog events, only 4 fell within range of any station, 
producing 5 station--trigger pairs (\cref{tab:triggers}).

\subsection{California Baseline}
\label{app:california}

We queried NCEDC for M3.5--5.0 events from the NCSN for 2020--2023, selecting the 
top 50 by magnitude to maximise signal quality. The lower magnitude bound ensures 
clear arrivals above noise, while the upper bound avoids instrument saturation and complex 
waveforms from very large events. Three-component stations are required because 
PhaseNet takes three channels as input. For each event, 120\,s three-component 
broadband waveforms were downloaded from up to 8 BK-network stations within 
$200$km of the epicentre via NCEDC. Ground truth picks were taken from the NCEDC 
catalog, ensuring picks and recordings refer to the same events. Phase variants 
(Pg, Pn, Sg, Sn) were mapped to P or S respectively, and only picks for 
station--event pairs with successful waveform downloads were retained, yielding 273 
P-wave and 12 S-wave ground truth picks across 325 waveforms.

\subsection{Waveform Preprocessing}
\label{app:preprocessing}

All waveforms (California and Kazakhstan) were preprocessed identically before 
inference: linear detrending, 5\% cosine tapering at both ends, bandpass filtering 
at 1--45\,Hz (Butterworth), and per-channel amplitude normalisation (handled 
internally by SeisBench). The 120\,s window starts 10\,s before the event origin 
time. The first three steps were applied using ObsPy prior to passing waveforms 
to PhaseNet.

\subsection{Pick Matching and Metrics}
\label{app:matching}

A PhaseNet pick is matched to the nearest catalog arrival of the same phase type 
(P or S) within 0.5\,s. Unmatched picks are classified as false positives. ECE is 
computed over 10 confidence bins spanning the 0.3--1.0 detection threshold range 
(bin width 0.07). AUROC summarises the binary task of distinguishing correct picks 
from false positives using confidence score as the discriminating variable. Both 
metrics are computed for P-wave picks only, as the S-wave asymmetry between regions 
(12 picks in California versus 74 in Kazakhstan) makes cross-region S-wave comparison 
unreliable.


\section{HMM Specification and Sensitivity Analysis}
\label{app:hmm}


\subsection{Emission Parameter Estimation}
\label{app:emission}

Each station's quiet-period emission rate $\lambda_Q$ is estimated as the mean weekly M$\geq$3.0 count, excluding the 4 weeks following each M$\geq$4.5 trigger event to 
avoid contamination by aftershock activity. For stations where the resulting mean is near zero, a floor of $\lambda_Q = 0.05$ is applied. This is necessary because a Poisson distribution with rate exactly zero assigns zero probability to any nonzero count, which would cause the forward algorithm to assign zero likelihood to weeks with observed events and break posterior inference.

\Cref{tab:emission_params} reports the estimated $\lambda_Q$ values for all 11 
stations. The stations divide into three groups: northern stations (ABKAR, AKTO, BORK, 
BVAR) with near-zero background rates, a middle group (KURK, MAKZ, MKAR, OTUK) with 
moderate activity, and southern stations (KKAR, KNDC, PDGK) with substantially higher 
background rates. This heterogeneity motivates station-specific estimation: a single 
regional rate would either overestimate the background at quiet stations or 
underestimate it at active ones, degrading the HMM's ability to detect anomalous weeks.

\subsection{Active Onset Detection Check}
\label{app:multiplier_check}

To verify that catalog injection is necessary, we compare the Poisson likelihood of the observed M$\geq$3.0 count at each trigger week under the Quiet and Active emission rates. If $P(n_t \mid \lambda_A) > P(n_t \mid\lambda_Q)$, the emission model alone would correctly favour Active. Otherwise, injection is required.

At 4 of 5 trigger weeks, the observed count favours Quiet over Active. The exception is OTUK, where the background rate is so low ($\lambda_Q = 0.14$) that even a count of 1 slightly favours Active. The most striking case is KNDC: the M5.1 event at week 125 produced an observed count of 3, against $\lambda_Q = 2.81$ and $\lambda_A = 22.47$. The likelihood $P(3 \mid \lambda_Q) = 0.223$ far exceeds $P(3 \mid \lambda_A) \approx 0$, meaning the emission model would classify that week as Quiet, missing the M5.1 event entirely. This confirms that Active onset 
cannot be reliably inferred from M$\geq$3.0 counts alone, justifying the hard injection approach described in \cref{sec:regime_model}.

\subsection{Transition Parameter Sensitivity}
\label{app:hmm_sensitivity}

The transition matrix parameters ($a_D = 0.35$, $d_Q = 0.18$, $d_A = 0.07$) are set 
from domain knowledge rather than estimated from data. To assess robustness to these 
choices, we re-run the forward algorithm under six perturbations: $a_D$ doubled (0.70) 
and halved (0.175), $d_Q$ doubled (0.36) and halved (0.09), and $k_A$ doubled (16) 
and halved (4).

\Cref{tab:sensitivity} reports recovery times (weeks until $\pi_t(Q) > 0.9$) for 
each triggered station under each configuration. At baseline, KNDC and PDGK (M5.1) 
both recover in 7 weeks, within the 4--8 week range predicted by Omori's law for 
M4.5--5.0 events. PDGK (M5.0) recovers in 3 weeks because post-event counts returned 
to background almost immediately. OTUK takes 12 weeks because its near-zero background 
and Decaying rates ($\lambda_Q = 0.14$, $\lambda_D = 0.21$) are difficult to 
distinguish, requiring many weeks of near-zero counts before $\pi_t(Q)$ exceeds 0.9. 
KKAR does not recover before the study period ends because its trigger occurs at week 
200 of 209, reflecting data truncation rather than a model failure.

Across all perturbations, the qualitative regime sequence following a large event is 
unchanged: Active, then Decaying, then Quiet. Recovery times shift predictably with 
the transition parameters but remain within a physically plausible range (3--19 weeks). 
The parameter with the most influence is $d_Q$, which directly controls the 
Decaying-to-Quiet transition rate: doubling it accelerates recovery, halving it 
extends it. $a_D$ and $k_A$ affect primarily the short Active phase and shift recovery 
times by at most 0--3 weeks from baseline. The untriggered stations maintain 
$\pi_t(\text{Quiet}) = 1.0$ throughout under all perturbations, since Active is 
entered only via forced catalog injection.

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{$\gamma$}{Gamma} Sensitivity}
\label{app:sensitivity}

The sensitivity parameter $\gamma$ controls the 
balance between confidence scores and regime-based penalty: smaller values make the regime penalty weaker, 
allowing confidence to dominate; larger values increase the penalty on data-sparse regimes. The sensitivity parameter $\gamma = 5$ was chosen as it produces a meaningful separation between regimes. At $\gamma = 5$, Active and Decaying regimes have posterior standard deviations of $\sigma_A \approx 0.123$ and $\sigma_D \approx 0.111$ respectively (\cref{tab:beta_final}), giving regime penalties of approximately $1 - 5 \times 0.12 = 0.40$. The Quiet regime, with $\sigma_Q \approx 0.038$, retains a penalty of approximately $1 - 5 \times 0.04 = 0.81$.  This separation in penalty ensures the framework penalises picks from data-sparse regimes substantially more than those from the well-observed Quiet regime.

\section{Deferral Framework Details}
\label{app:deferral}

\subsection{Beta-Bernoulli Convergence}

\Cref{fig:beta_convergence} shows the evolution of the reliability estimate $E[\theta_r]$ and posterior uncertainty $\sigma_r$ for each regime over the 190 Kazakhstan picks. The Beta-Bernoulli model is initialised with $\text{Beta}(2, 2)$ priors for all three regimes and updated sequentially using soft regime assignment. \Cref{tab:beta_final} reports the final posterior parameters.

\begin{table}[h]
    \centering
    \caption{Beta-Bernoulli posterior parameters after processing all 190 Kazakhstan picks.}
    \label{tab:beta_final}
    \small
    \begin{tabular}{@{}lccc@{}}
        \toprule
        Regime & $E[\theta_r]$ & $\sigma_r$ & Eff.\ samples \\
        \midrule
        Quiet & 0.425 & 0.038 & 164.6 \\
        Active & 0.600 & 0.123 & 11.0 \\
        Decaying & 0.400 & 0.111 & 14.4 \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}

The Quiet regime accumulates the vast majority of observations (164.6 effective samples), yielding a narrow posterior ($\sigma_Q = 0.038$). Active and Decaying regimes remain data-sparse (11.0 and 14.4 effective samples respectively), with posterior standard deviations approximately $3\times$ larger. This asymmetry drives the deferral score: $\sigma_t$ increases during Active and Decaying periods, suppressing confidence and triggering deferral.

\subsection{Threshold Sensitivity}

\Cref{fig:threshold_sweep} shows system accuracy as a function of deferral rate for both methods. At the 25\% operating point, confidence-only defers the 48 picks with lowest PhaseNet confidence, achieving 0.651 system accuracy. The combined score defers the 48 picks with the lowest regime-aware scores, achieving 0.619. Confidence-only maintains a slight accuracy advantage across most deferral rates because it directly targets the picks most likely to be incorrect, whereas the combined score prioritises regime-uncertain picks regardless of individual confidence.

\subsection{Analyst Accuracy Sensitivity}

\Cref{fig:analyst_sensitivity} shows system accuracy as a function of analyst accuracy for both methods. Confidence-only deferral exceeds the no-deferral baseline when analyst accuracy exceeds 8.3\%. The combined score requires analyst accuracy above 20.8\%. The higher breakeven for the combined score reflects the fact that it defers some high-confidence picks during uncertain regimes, picks that may already be correct, meaning the analyst must be reasonably accurate to improve on the automated decision.
