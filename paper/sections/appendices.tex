\subsection{Event Catalog}
\label{app:catalog}

The ISC catalog was queried for M$\geq$3.0 events in Central Asia (41--55$^\circ$N, 55--85$^\circ$E) for 2020--2023, yielding 6{,}763 events. The ISC is preferred over the USGS (United States Geological Survey) NEIC (National Earthquake Information Center) because it integrates phase data from regional agencies including KNDC, giving more accurate hypocentral solutions for Central Asian events. The M$\geq$3.0 threshold sits slightly below the network's estimated completeness magnitude of M3.5, preserving the background seismicity rate information the HMM needs to distinguish Quiet, Active, and Decaying regimes. The magnitude distribution is heavily right-skewed: 5{,}484 events with M$\geq$3.0, 98 with M$\geq$4.0, 12 with M$\geq$4.5, and 3 with M$\geq$5.0.

Regime transitions were triggered by M$\geq$4.5 events within 200\,km of a station. The M$\geq$4.5 threshold is chosen because earthquakes at this magnitude reliably produce sustained aftershock sequences, while smaller events may trigger isolated aftershocks but not the week-scale elevated seismicity needed to inform HMM regime transitions. The 200\,km radius is consistent with the spatial footprint of aftershock sequences for M4.5--5.0 mainshocks in the region. Of the 12 qualifying catalog events, only 4 fell within range of any station, producing 5 station--trigger pairs (\cref{tab:triggers}). The remaining 7 stations had no nearby M$\geq$4.5 events and stayed in the Quiet regime throughout, providing a natural untriggered control group.

\subsection{California Baseline}
\label{app:california}

We queried NCEDC for M3.5--5.0 events from the Northern California Seismic Network (NCSN) for 2020--2023, selecting the top 50 by magnitude to maximise signal quality. The M3.5--5.0 window mirrors the Kazakhstan catalog, ensuring any performance gap is attributable to geographic transfer rather than differences in earthquake size. The lower magnitude bound ensures clear arrivals above noise, while the upper bound avoids instrument saturation. Three-component stations are required because PhaseNet uses all three channels to discriminate P- and S-wave arrivals. For each event, 120\,s three-component broadband waveforms were downloaded from up to 8 BK-network stations within 2$^\circ$ of the epicentre via NCEDC. Ground truth picks were taken from the NCEDC catalog, the same source as the waveforms, ensuring picks and recordings refer to the same events. Phase variants (Pg, Pn, Sg, Sn) were mapped to P or S respectively, and only picks for station--event pairs with successful waveform downloads were retained, yielding 273 P-wave and 12 S-wave ground truth picks across 325 waveforms. PhaseNet's \texttt{original} pre-trained weights were used rather than the \texttt{stead} weights, since the NCSN training data originates from a single tectonic environment, making the transfer to Kazakhstan interpretable as a clean geographic shift.

\subsection{Waveform Preprocessing}
\label{app:preprocessing}

All waveforms (California and Kazakhstan) were preprocessed identically before inference:
%
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Linear detrending
    \item 5\% cosine tapering at both ends
    \item Bandpass filtering, 1--45\,Hz (Butterworth)
    \item Per-channel amplitude normalisation (handled internally by SeisBench)
\end{enumerate}
%
The 120\,s window starts 10\,s before the event origin time, capturing pre-event noise alongside both P and S arrivals. Steps 1--3 were applied using ObsPy prior to passing waveforms to PhaseNet.

\subsection{Pick Matching and Metrics}
\label{app:matching}

A PhaseNet pick is matched to the nearest catalog arrival of the same phase type (P or S) within 0.5\,s. Unmatched picks are classified as false positives. The 0.5\,s window is wider than the 0.1\,s criterion in the original PhaseNet evaluation~\citep{zhu2019phasenet} because ISC arrival times are independently processed and may carry small systematic timing offsets relative to PhaseNet's output. All evaluation metrics are reported for P-wave picks only: Kazakhstan has 74 S-wave ground truth picks versus 12 in California, an asymmetry too large for a meaningful cross-region S-wave comparison. ECE is computed over 10 confidence bins spanning the 0.3--1.0 detection threshold range (bin width 0.07). AUROC summarises the binary task of distinguishing correct picks from false positives using confidence score as the discriminating variable.

\section{HMM Specification and Sensitivity Analysis}
\label{app:hmm}


\paragraph{Emission parameter estimation.} Each station's quiet-period emission rate $\lambda_Q$ is estimated as the mean weekly M3+ count, excluding the 4 weeks following each M4.5+ trigger event to avoid contamination by aftershock activity. For stations where the resulting mean is near zero, a floor of $\lambda_Q = 0.05$ is applied. This is necessary because a Poisson distribution with rate exactly zero assigns zero probability to any nonzero count, which would cause the forward algorithm to assign zero likelihood to weeks with observed events and break posterior inference.

The Quiet regime accumulates the vast majority of observations (164.6 effective samples), yielding a narrow posterior ($\sigma_Q = 0.038$). Active and Decaying regimes remain data-sparse (11.0 and 14.4 effective samples respectively), with posterior standard deviations approximately $3\times$ larger. This asymmetry drives the deferral score: $\sigma_t$ increases during Active and Decaying periods, suppressing confidence and triggering deferral.

\subsection{Threshold Sensitivity}

\Cref{fig:threshold_sweep} shows system accuracy as a function of deferral rate for both methods. At the 25\% operating point, confidence-only defers the 48 picks with lowest PhaseNet confidence, achieving 0.651 system accuracy. The combined score defers the 48 picks with the lowest regime-aware scores, achieving 0.619. Confidence-only maintains a slight accuracy advantage across most deferral rates because it directly targets the picks most likely to be incorrect, whereas the combined score prioritises regime-uncertain picks regardless of individual confidence.

\subsection{Analyst Accuracy Sensitivity}

\Cref{fig:analyst_sensitivity} shows system accuracy as a function of analyst accuracy for both methods. Confidence-only deferral exceeds the no-deferral baseline when analyst accuracy exceeds 8.3\%. The combined score requires analyst accuracy above 20.8\%. The higher breakeven for the combined score reflects the fact that it defers some high-confidence picks during uncertain regimes, picks that may already be correct, meaning the analyst must be reasonably accurate to improve on the automated decision.
