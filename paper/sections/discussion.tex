\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Small regime-stratified samples.} The Active and Decaying performance analysis relies on a single station (PDGK) with small samples per regime. We frame these results as suggestive, not statistically conclusive.

\paragraph{Single target region.} We demonstrate the framework on one distribution shift (California $\to$ Kazakhstan). The HMM parameters and deferral threshold would need recalibration for each new deployment region.

\paragraph{Small evaluation dataset.} The entire Kazakhstan evaluation is based on 190 PhaseNet picks against 202 P-wave and 74 S-wave ISC arrivals, limiting the statistical power of finer-grained analyses.

\paragraph{Simulated analyst.} The human-in-the-loop evaluation uses a synthetic analyst with fixed accuracy (95\%) and deterministic prioritisation, which does not capture the operational complexity of real-time analyst review.

\paragraph{Retrospective ground truth.} The Beta-Bernoulli model updates on every pick's correctness, available retrospectively but not in real-time deployment where only deferred picks receive analyst feedback. This creates a potential selection bias, and extending the update mechanism to handle partial feedback is a direction for future work.

\paragraph{Catalog dependence.} Regime triggers require a functioning earthquake catalog. In the most data-sparse regions, the catalog may be incomplete, potentially missing events that should trigger Active.

\paragraph{Weekly resolution.} The HMM operates at weekly granularity, which may be too coarse to capture rapid regime transitions in the first hours after a mainshock.

\paragraph{Accuracy vs.\ targeting trade-off.} The combined score achieves lower raw system accuracy than confidence-only at matched deferral rates. Practitioners who prioritise accuracy over temporal targeting may prefer confidence-only deferral.

\paragraph{Recall vs.\ precision failure modes.} PhaseNet's dominant failure mode under distribution shift is missed detections, but the Beta-Bernoulli model tracks only precision. It cannot detect picks that PhaseNet fails to make, since missed detections have no timestamp to align with regime posteriors.

\paragraph{No formal safety guarantees.} The deferral threshold targets a deferral rate, not an error rate or safety level. Integrating methods such as conformal prediction to provide finite-sample coverage guarantees is a direction for future work.

\paragraph{Discrete regime assumption.} The HMM assumes three discrete states. Intermediate states, overlapping aftershock sequences, or non-Markov dynamics are not captured.

\paragraph{Exchangeability within regimes.} The Beta-Bernoulli model treats all picks within a regime as exchangeable, ignoring station-specific effects, magnitude-dependent performance, and signal-to-noise variation.

\paragraph{Fixed HMM parameters.} The transition matrix and emission rates are set once from domain knowledge and do not adapt over time. Over longer deployment timescales, seismicity patterns could change and the system would not detect this without manual re-estimation.

\paragraph{Ground truth quality.} The ISC Reviewed Bulletin is treated as ground truth, but ISC picks have their own measurement errors and the bulletin's completeness varies by region.

\paragraph{Baseline comparisons.} We compare against confidence-only deferral and no deferral. More sophisticated methods such as ensemble disagreement or learned rejectors require either architectural modifications to PhaseNet or labelled deployment-domain data, neither of which is available in our setting.

\subsection{Do the Experiments Support the Claims?}
\label{sec:self_eval}

We cannot claim the regime signal \emph{causes} better deferral. The comparison is observational, not ablated in a controlled experiment. What we show is that the two methods defer systematically different picks, and that the combined score's pattern aligns with operationally relevant regime structure.

The baselines are appropriate: confidence-only deferral is the standard approach a practitioner would use, and it is a strong baseline that wins on raw accuracy. We do not compare against more sophisticated methods (e.g., learned rejectors, ensemble disagreement) because these require either labelled deployment data or architectural modifications to PhaseNet, neither of which is available in our setting.

System accuracy captures overall correctness, and regime-stratified deferral rates capture temporal targeting. We report both rather than selecting the metric that favours our method.

\subsection{Broader Implications for Safety-Critical ML}
\label{sec:implications}

This work illustrates a general principle: when a model cannot self-diagnose failure under distribution shift, \emph{external context about the deployment environment} can provide the reliability signal that internal uncertainty cannot. The specific context here is seismic regime, but the architecture applies wherever deployment conditions are structured and observable, these conditions affect model reliability systematically, and the model's own uncertainty fails to reflect this. Examples include medical imaging across hospitals with different equipment profiles, autonomous vehicles across geographies with different road conditions, or environmental monitoring systems deployed across ecosystems.
