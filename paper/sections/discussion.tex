\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}

We organise limitations into three categories: constraints on the evidence base, assumptions that would matter in operational deployment, and inherent boundaries of the framework's scope.

\paragraph{Data and generalisability.} The entire Kazakhstan evaluation is based on 190 PhaseNet picks against 202 P-wave and 74 S-wave ISC arrivals, limiting the statistical power of finer-grained analyses. The Active and Decaying performance analysis relies on a single station (PDGK) with small samples per regime, and we frame these results as suggestive, not statistically conclusive. We demonstrate the framework on one distribution shift (California $\to$ Kazakhstan), and the HMM parameters and deferral threshold would need recalibration for each new deployment region. We compare against confidence-only deferral and no deferral, as more sophisticated methods such as ensemble disagreement or learned rejectors require either architectural modifications to PhaseNet or labelled deployment-domain data, neither of which is available in our setting.

\paragraph{Evaluation and modelling assumptions.} The Beta-Bernoulli model updates on every pick's correctness, available retrospectively but not in real-time deployment where only deferred picks receive analyst feedback. This creates a potential selection bias, and extending the update mechanism to handle partial feedback is a direction for future work. The human-in-the-loop evaluation uses a synthetic analyst with fixed accuracy (95\%) and deterministic prioritisation, which does not capture the operational complexity of real-time analyst review. The ISC Reviewed Bulletin is treated as ground truth, but ISC picks have their own measurement errors and the bulletin's completeness varies by region. Regime triggers require a functioning earthquake catalogue, and in the most data-sparse regions the catalogue may be incomplete, potentially missing events that should trigger Active. The HMM operates at weekly granularity, which may be too coarse to capture rapid regime transitions in the first hours after a mainshock. The HMM also assumes three discrete states, treats all picks within a regime as exchangeable (ignoring station-specific effects, magnitude-dependent performance, and signal-to-noise variation), and uses a fixed transition matrix and emission rates set once from domain knowledge that do not adapt over time.

\paragraph{Inherent trade-offs.} The combined score achieves lower raw system accuracy than confidence-only at matched deferral rates. Practitioners who prioritise accuracy over temporal targeting may prefer confidence-only deferral. PhaseNet's dominant failure mode under distribution shift is missed detections, but the Beta-Bernoulli model tracks only precision because the framework processes only PhaseNet's output and has no mechanism to detect arrivals PhaseNet missed. The deferral threshold targets a deferral rate, not an error rate or safety level. Integrating methods such as conformal prediction to provide finite-sample coverage guarantees is a direction for future work.

\medskip
We cannot claim the regime signal \emph{causes} better deferral. The comparison is observational, not ablated in a controlled experiment. What we show is that the two methods defer systematically different picks, and that the combined score's pattern aligns with operationally relevant regime structure. We report both system accuracy and regime-stratified deferral rates rather than selecting the metric that favours our method.
