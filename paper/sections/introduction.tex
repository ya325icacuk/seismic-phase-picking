\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Safety-critical machine learning systems are increasingly deployed under distribution shift: a model trained in one setting is used in another, and its performance may degrade without warning. The standard defence is to use the model's own uncertainty estimates to flag unreliable predictions, deferring low-confidence outputs to a human expert. This assumes the model knows what it does not know. When this assumption fails, the system produces confident but incorrect outputs with no internal signal to trigger human review.

We study this failure concretely through seismic phase picking, the task of identifying earthquake wave arrivals in continuous seismograms. Earthquakes 
generate two primary wave types: P-waves (compressional, faster) and S-waves (shear, slower), whose arrival times are used to locate earthquakes and assess their magnitude. PhaseNet \citep{zhu2019phasenet}, trained on Northern California data, sees its P-wave detection rate collapse from 83.9\% to 35.1\% when deployed to Kazakhstan. Despite this collapse, mean confidence for matched picks is nearly unchanged (0.749 vs.\ 0.747), and P-wave AUROC for correct/incorrect discrimination is virtually identical across regions (0.699 vs.\ 0.701). Confidence-based deferral has no signal that anything has changed. Alternative deferral methods require either informative confidence scores as a deferral signal or plenty of local labelled data, neither of which is available in our setting.

We propose a regime-aware deferral framework that extends model self-assessment with external environmental context. An HMM tracks the seismic environment (quiet background, active aftershock sequences, gradual decay) and a Beta-Bernoulli model tracks how much evidence exists about PhaseNet's reliability in each regime. The combined deferral score weights PhaseNet's confidence against regime uncertainty, routing picks from poorly-observed regimes to a human analyst. Our contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item An empirical demonstration that PhaseNet's confidence scores are uninformative under distribution shift: the model fails silently, with no internal signal to trigger human review. This establishes that standard uncertainty quantification tools are insufficient for deferral in this setting and motivates the need for an external reliability signal.
    \item A regime-aware monitoring framework using an HMM to track the seismic environment, providing a structured external signal that model confidence cannot. This enables deferral decisions that reflect when the deployment environment is most uncertain, rather than when individual picks are most uncertain.
    \item A Bayesian deferral mechanism combining regime uncertainty with pick-level confidence, which concentrates deferrals during seismically active periods rather than spreading them uniformly across time. This directs scarce analyst time toward the periods of greatest environmental disturbance, at a marginal cost in system accuracy relative to confidence-only deferral.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Deep Learning Phase Picking and Distribution Shift}
\label{sec:bg_picking}

Deep learning phase pickers trained on well-instrumented networks perform well within their training distribution but degrade when deployed to seismically distinct or data-sparse regions. PhaseNet \citep{zhu2019phasenet} is a prominent 
example: a U-Net convolutional neural network trained on ${\sim}780{,}000$ Northern California waveforms that produces continuous P-wave, S-wave, and noise probability traces, widely deployed via SeisBench \citep{woollam2022seisbench}. \citet{Munchmeyer2022} show that performance degrades systematically with tectonic dissimilarity and network sparsity, and \citet{ovadia2019uncertainty} show that confidence scores are poorly calibrated under dataset shift. Neither addresses what to do when a picker is deployed to a region that is both tectonically dissimilar and data-sparse, where degradation is most likely and retraining data is unavailable. We confirm both findings for PhaseNet in Kazakhstan (\cref{sec:results}) and address the open question of how to flag unreliable predictions when confidence itself is uninformative.

\subsection{Selective Prediction and Learning to Defer}
\label{sec:bg_defer}

Methods for selective prediction and learning to defer address model unreliability by routing uncertain predictions to a human, but require either informative confidence scores or labelled deployment-domain data. Selective prediction \citep{chow1970optimum, geifman2017selective} uses model confidence directly as a deferral signal; the learning-to-defer framework \citep{madras2018predict, mozannar2020consistent} relaxes this by training a learned rejector. Both methods fail in our setting: PhaseNet's confidence is uninformative under shift (\cref{sec:confidence}) and labelled Kazakhstan data is too scarce to train a rejector. The deferral signal must therefore come from outside the model, from observable structure in the deployment environment that is independent of model self-assessment.

\subsection{Seismic Regime Modelling}
\label{sec:bg_regime}

Aftershock sequences follow Omori's law \citep{omori1894aftershocks, utsu1995centenary}, decaying as a power law from a mainshock through elevated activity back to background seismicity. The ETAS model \citep{ogata1988etas} captures this process in detail but requires extended observation of both active and quiet periods \citep{naylor2023bayesian}, which our deployment setting cannot provide. Poisson HMMs offer an alternative, modelling the seismic environment as discrete hidden states that capture the structured transitions of the aftershock cycle. \citet{orfanogiannaki2010poisson} demonstrate that a Poisson HMM on weekly earthquake counts detects regime shifts that traditional rate-change analysis misses, and \citet{felix2022poisson} independently reach the 
same modelling choice for a different region. However, existing work uses regime models to characterise or forecast seismicity patterns rather than as reliability signals for deployed models. We repurpose seismic regime modelling to assess model reliability under deployment conditions, providing an external deferral signal that confidence scores cannot.
