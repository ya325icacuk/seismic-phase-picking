\subsection{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem}

PhaseNet produces a pick $p$ with confidence $c \in [0, 1]$ at a station during week $t$. A deferral function $d(p) \in \{\text{accept}, \text{defer}\}$ must decide whether to trust the automated pick or route it for analyst review. The challenge is that $c$ is uninformative under distribution shift (demonstrated empirically in \cref{sec:confidence}). We require an additional signal from outside the model, and a way to combine it with $c$ so that the deferral decision reflects both pick-level confidence and environmental context.

\subsection{Seismic Regime Model}
\label{sec:regime_model}

To provide this external signal, the HMM classifies each station's current seismic environment into one of three regimes, based on the seismic cycle described in \cref{sec:bg_regime}. It does not detect earthquakes directly. Instead, it estimates whether each station is still experiencing elevated seismicity following a large event, or has returned to background levels.

We define three hidden states: Quiet ($Q$), Active ($A$), and Decaying ($D$). At each week $t$, the HMM observes the count of M3+ earthquakes within 200\,km of a given station. 


These counts are modelled as Poisson-distributed with station-specific emission rates. The Quiet rate $\lambda_Q$ is estimated from weeks without nearby M4.5+ events. The Active rate is a multiple of the Quiet rate: $\lambda_A = k_A \cdot \lambda_Q$, with $k_A = 8$, within the established 5--10$\times$ aftershock rate range \citep{reasenberg1989earthquake}. The Decaying rate $\lambda_D = k_D \cdot \lambda_Q$ uses $k_D = 1.5$, producing recovery times of 4--8 weeks for M4.5--5.0 aftershock sequences, consistent with Omori's law \citep{utsu1995centenary}. Station-specific estimation details (aftershock exclusion windows and a floor value for low-seismicity stations) are provided in \cref{app:hmm}.

The transition matrix encodes seismological domain knowledge:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{T} = \begin{pmatrix}
        1 - \epsilon & \epsilon & 0 \\
        0 & 1 - a_D & a_D \\
        d_Q & d_A & 1 - d_Q - d_A
    \end{pmatrix}
    \label{eq:transition}
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon \approx 0$. The Active state is not inferred from observations but assigned directly from the earthquake catalogue when a M4.5+ event occurs within 200\,km of a station. $a_D = 0.35$ is the Active-to-Decaying transition rate, giving an expected Active duration of approximately 3 weeks, consistent with the aftershock phase for M4.5--5.0 events. $d_Q = 0.18$ is the Decaying-to-Quiet recovery rate. $d_A = 0.07$ is the Decaying-to-Active reactivation probability. Together, $d_Q$ and $d_A$ give an expected Decaying duration of approximately 4 weeks, consistent with Omori's law decay timescales \citep{utsu1995centenary}.

All stations begin with a Quiet prior ($\pi_0 = [1, 0, 0]$). The forward algorithm \citep{rabiner1989hmm} updates this into regime posteriors $\pi_t(r) = P(\text{regime} = r \mid \text{observations up to week } t)$ for each station independently at weekly resolution.

\subsection{Beta-Bernoulli Reliability Tracking}
\label{sec:beta_bernoulli}

With the regime posteriors from \cref{sec:regime_model} in hand, we now estimate PhaseNet's reliability in each regime. For each $r \in \{Q, A, D\}$, we maintain a Beta-distributed estimate of this reliability, the probability that a pick made during regime $r$ is correct:
\begin{equation}
    \theta_r \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha_r, \beta_r).
\end{equation}
This is conjugate Bayesian updating. All three regimes are initialised with $\text{Beta}(2, 2)$ priors, a weak prior centered at 0.5 with an effective sample size of 4, so that even sparse regimes can shift the estimate.

Each pick's outcome updates all three regimes' Beta parameters, weighted by $\pi_t(r)$. Picks are processed in chronological order. Each pick receives its deferral score (\cref{sec:combined_score}) from the current Beta parameters, and the Beta parameters are updated only afterwards. For a correct pick:
\begin{equation}
    \alpha_r \leftarrow \alpha_r + \pi_t(r), \qquad \beta_r \leftarrow \beta_r \quad \forall\, r \in \{Q, A, D\}
\end{equation}
and for an incorrect pick:
\begin{equation}
    \alpha_r \leftarrow \alpha_r, \qquad \beta_r \leftarrow \beta_r + \pi_t(r) \quad \forall\, r \in \{Q, A, D\}.
\end{equation}

As the Beta parameters accumulate observations, we need a measure of how much we can trust each regime's reliability estimate. The posterior standard deviation $\sigma_r = \text{std}(\text{Beta}(\alpha_r, \beta_r))$ captures how much evidence we have about our estimate of PhaseNet's reliability in each regime: high $\sigma_r$ indicates fewer observations, low $\sigma_r$ indicates more. Since the regime at week $t$ is not known with certainty, the $\sigma_r$ combine into:
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_t = \sum_{r} \pi_t(r) \cdot \sigma_r.
    \label{eq:sigma_t}
\end{equation}
This quantity drives the deferral score defined in \cref{sec:combined_score}.

\subsection{Design Rationale}
\label{sec:design_rationale}

The deferral framework combines three signals, each contributing information the others lack. PhaseNet's confidence answers ``which picks within the same regime are most likely correct?'' The HMM (\cref{sec:regime_model}) answers ``what is happening in the seismic environment right now?'' but knows nothing about PhaseNet. The Beta-Bernoulli model (\cref{sec:beta_bernoulli}) answers ``how reliable is PhaseNet in that environment?''

The Beta-Bernoulli model produces two quantities per regime: a reliability estimate $E[\theta_r]$ and its posterior standard deviation $\sigma_r$. We defer based on $\sigma_r$, which shrinks as the effective sample size $(\alpha_r + \beta_r)$ grows. $\sigma_r$ distinguishes regimes with similar reliability estimates but different amounts of data: it is high when data is sparse and low when data is abundant. This is why we use $\sigma_r$ rather than $E[\theta_r]$: the mean reflects measured performance, but $\sigma_r$ reflects how much we should trust that measurement. Picks are therefore deferred from regimes where the reliability estimate rests on few observations: absence of evidence about PhaseNet's reliability is treated as grounds for human review.

\subsection{Combined Deferral Score}
\label{sec:combined_score}

Having established $\sigma_r$ as the regime-level deferral signal, we now combine all three signals into a single score:
\begin{equation}
    s(p) = c \cdot \max\!\big(0,\; 1 - \gamma \cdot \sigma_t\big)
    \label{eq:deferral_score}
\end{equation}
The sensitivity parameter $\gamma$ controls how aggressively regime uncertainty suppresses PhaseNet's confidence. When $\sigma_t$ is small (data-rich regimes with narrow posteriors), the score preserves most of the confidence signal. When $\sigma_t$ is large (data-sparse regimes), the score is suppressed toward zero. When $\gamma \cdot \sigma_t \geq 1$, the score is exactly zero regardless of PhaseNet's confidence, guaranteeing deferral for all picks in highly uncertain regimes. Given the score, the pick is deferred if $s(p) < \tau$, where $\tau$ is set to achieve a target deferral rate. We use $\gamma = 5$ throughout. The choice of $\gamma$ and $\tau$ is discussed in \cref{sec:setup}.
