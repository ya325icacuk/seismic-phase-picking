\subsection{PhaseNet Performance Under Distribution Shift}
\label{sec:shift}

\Cref{tab:shift} summarises PhaseNet's performance across both regions. The California results confirm that PhaseNet performs as published: 83.9\% P-wave detection with residuals approaching the 0.1\,s benchmark from \citet{zhu2019phasenet}. In Kazakhstan, P-wave detection falls to 35.1\%, a 48.8-percentage-point drop, with PhaseNet missing nearly two-thirds of arrivals that human analysts identified.

Critically, the P-wave false pick rate is comparable between regions (31.4\% vs.\ 34.9\%), indicating that PhaseNet's dominant failure mode under distribution shift is \emph{missed detections} rather than incorrect picks. Despite this performance collapse, mean confidence for matched picks is nearly unchanged (0.749 vs.\ 0.747).

\subsection{Confidence Scores Do Not Detect Distribution Shift}
\label{sec:confidence}

    \fbox{\parbox{0.9\columnwidth}{\centering\vspace{1.5cm}\textbf{Figure 3: TODO}\\[0.3em]
    Confidence histograms overlay\vspace{1.5cm}}}
    \caption{P-wave confidence distributions for California (blue) and Kazakhstan (red). Despite the detection rate collapse, the distributions are nearly indistinguishable.}
    \label{fig:confidence_overlap}
\end{figure}

\Cref{sec:shift} showed that PhaseNet's performance degrades sharply under distribution shift. We now ask whether its confidence scores provide any warning of this degradation.

P-wave ECE increases from 0.088 in California to 0.150 in Kazakhstan (\cref{tab:shift}). At the 0.7 confidence level, California picks are correct approximately 70\% of the time, but Kazakhstan picks only approximately 50\%. PhaseNet appears overconfident under distribution shift. To assess whether the issue goes beyond calibration, we turn to discrimination analysis.

P-wave AUROC is 0.699 in California versus 0.701 in Kazakhstan, virtually identical. The model distinguishes correct from incorrect picks with similar confidence in both regions, despite the performance drop documented in \cref{sec:shift}. Unlike calibration, which could in principle be corrected by post-hoc rescaling, discrimination cannot be improved without changing the model's internal representations. A confidence-based deferral system operating in Kazakhstan would have no signal that anything had changed (\cref{fig:confidence_overlap}).

    \label{fig:regime_posteriors}

The HMM correctly detects all five M$\geq$4.5 triggers with $\pi_t(A) = 1.0$. Recovery to $\pi_t(Q) > 0.9$ takes 7 weeks at KNDC and PDGK following the M5.1 event, 3 weeks at PDGK for the M5.0, and 12 weeks at OTUK. KKAR, triggered by the M4.8 event in the final weeks of the study period, does not recover before the study window ends.

The 7 untriggered stations maintain $\pi_t(Q) = 1.0$ throughout, with zero false activations (\cref{fig:regime_posteriors}). A sensitivity analysis across 7 parameter configurations shows recovery times varying between 3 and 19 weeks (\cref{app:hmm}).

Active detection and untriggered stability are guaranteed by design (hard injection with $\epsilon \approx 0$), not learned from data. The HMM's genuine contribution is estimating the \emph{decay timeline}, how many weeks of heightened oversight each station needs after a large event. This is the component tested by the sensitivity analysis.

\paragraph{Regime-stratified performance.} Time-aligning the 190 Kazakhstan picks to regime posteriors shows 81.6\% fall in predominantly Quiet weeks, 5.8\% in Active, 7.9\% in Decaying, and 4.7\% in mixed-regime transitions. Only PDGK has ground-truth arrivals during Active (13 arrivals) and Decaying (11 arrivals) periods. At this station, precision varies across regimes (Active: 64\%, Quiet: 42\%, Decaying: 33\%), suggesting performance depends on environmental state. However, these sample sizes are small and the pattern should be interpreted as suggestive rather than statistically conclusive. The primary value of the regime model is not in predicting per-regime performance, but in providing the uncertainty signal ($\sigma_r$) that drives deferral. Regimes with few observations have high $\sigma_r$ by construction.

\Cref{tab:deferral} shows that confidence-only deferral achieves higher system accuracy (0.651 vs.\ 0.619) at matched deferral rates. This is expected: confidence-only directly optimises ``which individual picks are most likely wrong?'' and selects the lowest-confidence picks for human review.

The combined score's advantage lies in \emph{regime targeting} (\cref{tab:targeting}). It concentrates 90.9\% of deferrals during Active periods versus 36.4\% for confidence-only. This difference arises because PhaseNet's confidence carries almost no regime signal (mean confidence ranges only from 0.616 to 0.637 across all three regimes), so confidence-only deferral spreads deferrals near-uniformly across time.

The two methods answer different questions. Confidence-only asks: \emph{which picks are most likely wrong?} This is better for maximising accuracy at a fixed deferral budget. The combined score asks: \emph{when is the model operating in an environment where we have insufficient evidence about its reliability?} This is better for directing human attention to periods of greatest environmental uncertainty. For safety-critical deployment, we argue the second question is more operationally relevant: a monitoring agency needs to know \emph{when} the system requires oversight, not just which individual picks to double-check. We do not claim the combined score is universally better. The choice depends on operational priorities.

\subsection{Human-in-the-Loop Evaluation}
\label{sec:hitl}

Under capacity constraints (where the analyst can only review a fraction of deferred picks), the combined score's regime targeting means the analyst reviews Active and Decaying picks first, the periods of greatest environmental disturbance. Both methods saturate once capacity reaches approximately 25\% (all 48 deferred picks reviewed). At saturation, confidence-only achieves 0.651 and the combined score achieves 0.619. The combined score directs 20.8\% of analyst capacity to Active picks and 18.8\% to Decaying, compared with 8.3\% and 10.4\% for confidence-only.

% YASMIN: New paragraph from Step 7c (analyst accuracy sensitivity analysis)
\paragraph{Sensitivity to analyst accuracy.} The system accuracy numbers above assume 95\% analyst accuracy on deferred picks. Since this assumption is not empirically verified, we sweep analyst accuracy from 70\% to 99\%. Both deferral methods exceed the no-deferral baseline (0.432) at all plausible analyst accuracies. Confidence-only requires only $>$8.3\% analyst accuracy to beat the baseline, while the combined score requires $>$20.8\%. At a more conservative 85\% analyst accuracy, system accuracy drops to 0.625 (confidence-only) and 0.594 (combined). The advantage of deferral is robust to this assumption, though the absolute improvement scales with analyst quality. Full capacity curves and analyst sensitivity details are provided in \cref{app:deferral}.
